1. Eigen values and Eigen vectors

  • Sum of diagonal elements = Sum of eigen values.
\begin{equation} | A - \lambda I | = 0 ... Characteristic Equation \end{equation} \begin{equation} \lambda^3-[sum of diagonal elements]\lambda^2+[sum of minor of diagonal elements]\lambda-|A|=0 \end{equation} \begin{equation} \lambda=x,y,z \\ \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \left( \begin{matrix} a-\lambda & b & c \\ d & e-\lambda & f \\ g & h & i-\lambda \end{matrix} \right) \begin{matrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ x_3 \end{matrix} = \begin{matrix} 0\\ 0\\ 0 \end{matrix} \end{equation} \begin{equation} ax_1+bx_2+cx_3=0 \\ dx_1+ex_2+fx_3=0 \\ gx_1+hx_2+ix_3=0 \\ \end{equation}

2. Cayley-Hamilton Theorem

\begin{equation} | A - \lambda I | = 0 ... Characteristic Equation \\ \end{equation} \begin{equation} \lambda^3-[sum of diagonal elements]\lambda^2+[sum of minor of diagonal elements]\lambda-|A|=0 \end{equation}
  • Now put instead of λ.

\(A^3-[sum of diagonal elements]A^2+[sum of minor of diagonal elements]A-|A|=0\)

3. Similarity of Matrices

3.1. Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity of an Eignevalue

3.1.1. Algebraic Multiplicity

  • If an eignevalue λ1 of matrix A is repeated t times the t is called the algebraic multiplicity of λ1.

3.1.2. Geometric Multiplicity

  • If corresponding to an eigenvalue λ1, there are s linearly independent eigenvectors the s is called the geometric multiplicity of λ1

4. Diagonalising and Non-Diagonalising Matrix

4.1. Diagonalising a Given Matrix

4.1.1. (a) Distinct Eigenvalues

  • If all eignevalues are distinct the matrix is Diagonalisable
  • If \(X_1,X_2,X_3\) then \(M=[X_1, X_2, X_3]\) is the transforming matrix

  • If \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3\) are the distinct eigenvalues then the diagonal matrix D is

    \begin{matrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_3 \end{matrix}
  • \(M^{-1}AM=D\) This shows that M diagonalises A to diagonal matrix D.

4.1.2. (b) Repeated Eigenvalues

  1. If an eigenvalue λ is repeated 't' times then its algebraic multiplicity is 't'.
  2. If there are 's' linearly independent eigenvectors then the geometric multiplicity of λ is 's'.
  3. If \(t=s\) then the matrix is diagonalisable.
  4. Rest is same as above.

4.2. Non-Diagonalising Matrix

  • If the algebraic multiplicity of λ is not equal the geometric multiplicity, then the matrix A is not diagonalisable.
